Molecular Muscle
Physiology
Laboratory

We investigate the molecular and cellular mechanisms underlying skeletal muscle growth, repair, and aging — with the goal of developing novel therapies that prevent disability and restore function.

Our Work

Research Focus

Two primary research themes that together illuminate how exercise shapes muscle at the molecular level — and how that knowledge can be harnessed therapeutically.

01

Role of the α7β1 Integrin as a Mechanosensor & Modulator of Muscle Growth

The α7β1 integrin links laminin outside the muscle fiber to the actin cytoskeleton inside, serving as a critical mechanosensor. Our lab was the first to show that α7 integrin RNA and protein increase in both rodent and human skeletal muscle following eccentric exercise. Using transgenic and knockout mouse models, we demonstrated its role in protecting sarcolemmal integrity and facilitating hypertrophy.

Current efforts probe how the α7β1 complex drives strain-induced growth, and why this mechanism declines with aging and following disuse.

02

Development of cell- and extracellular vesicle (EV)-based strategies to recover skeletal muscle mass and strength after disuse

Perivascular stem/stromal cells — pericytes and mesenchymal stem cells — reside in the skeletal muscle niche and respond to mechanical stimuli. We documented exercise-induced expansion of these cells and demonstrated that transplanted pericytes can facilitate repair, myofiber growth, arteriogenesis, and strength recovery in mice.

Current efforts use single-cell RNA sequencing to map mechanosensitive mononuclear populations and develop extracellular vesicle (EV)-based therapies to restore aged muscle after immobilization.

🧬

Translational Impact

Our findings on pericyte-derived extracellular vesicles point toward cell-free therapeutics that could be frozen, stored, and administered to accelerate muscle recovery in injured and aging patients — addressing a critical unmet need in rehabilitation medicine. This work is funded by the National Institute of Arthritis and Musculoskeletal and Skin Diseases (NIAMS) at NIH.

Lab Opportunities

The Molecular Muscle Physiology Laboratory welcomes motivated students and researchers at all levels who are passionate about exercise biology, skeletal muscle physiology, and translational science.

We particularly encourage applications from students with backgrounds in kinesiology, cell biology, biochemistry, bioengineering, or related fields. Undergraduate researchers gain hands-on experience with cell culture, mouse models, molecular biology techniques, and data analysis.
